Signs your garage door opener needs repair

Openers rarely quit without warning. The symptoms below are the ones Metro Detroit homeowners call us about most, and each one points to a specific, fixable fault.

None of these mean you automatically need a new opener. A stripped gear, a worn trolley, a misaligned safety sensor, or a remote that needs reprogramming are all standard repairs we complete in one visit.

  • The door doesn't respond to the remote or wall button, or only works from a few feet away
  • The motor runs with a grinding or rattling noise but the door barely moves
  • Remotes or the keypad lose their programming, work intermittently, or lose range
  • The door starts to close, then reverses before it reaches the floor
  • The trolley slides along the rail but the door itself stays shut
  • The opener light blinks and the motor clicks without lifting the door

Chain, belt, screw, and direct drive: we repair all four

Every residential opener moves the door one of four ways, and each drive type wears in its own pattern. Knowing which one hangs in your garage tells us a lot before we even arrive.

Chain drive openers are the workhorses of Metro Detroit garages: affordable, durable, and a little noisy. Their weak points are chain slack, worn sprockets, and stripped drive gears, all of which we adjust or replace. Belt drive units run quietly, which makes them popular under bedrooms, but the belt itself can fray, glaze, or lose tension over years of cycles. Screw drive openers move the door with a long threaded rod and have few moving parts, but they are sensitive to Michigan's temperature swings and depend on fresh lubrication to keep from binding in a cold snap. Direct drive models move the motor itself along a stationary chain, so there is very little to wear, though sensors, remotes, and travel settings still fail the same way they do on every other type.

Whichever type you own, the diagnosis follows the same path: we test the motor, the drive, the safety sensors, and the controls, then fix the part that actually failed instead of guessing.

Repair or replace? An honest answer

Most opener problems end in a repair, not a replacement. Stripped gears, worn trolleys, frayed belts, misaligned photo eyes, faulty wall buttons, and remotes that lost their code are all fixable for far less than the cost of a new unit.

We recommend replacement in a few specific situations: the motor itself has burned out, the same unit keeps failing repair after repair, parts for a discontinued model are no longer available, or an older opener lacks working safety reversal. If replacement really is the smarter spend, we'll say so plainly, walk you through quiet belt drive and heavy-duty chain drive options, and back the installation with the same workmanship guarantee as every repair. It's the same straight repair-versus-replace talk we bring to door repair throughout the house.

Garage Door Opener Repair — FAQs

My garage door opener won't respond to the remote or wall button. What should I check first?

Start with the simple things: a dead remote battery, the lock or vacation switch on the wall console, which disables remotes when it is on, and the outlet the opener plugs into. If those all check out and the door still won't respond, the fault is usually in the receiver board, the wall-button wiring, or the remotes themselves. We test each link in that chain and repair the one that failed.

Why is my opener grinding but the garage door won't move?

A grinding or rattling motor with a door that stays shut usually means the main drive gear has stripped or the trolley that pulls the door has worn out or disengaged. Stop running the opener once you hear grinding, because every cycle chews the gear further. Both repairs are jobs we can usually finish in a single visit with parts from the truck.

Why does my garage door start to close and then reverse back up?

That reversal is the opener's safety system doing its job, just too eagerly. The photo-eye sensors near the floor may be misaligned, blocked, or wired loose, or the travel and force settings may need adjustment. We realign the sensors, check the wiring, and reset the limits so the door closes fully and still reverses when something is actually in its path.

Should I repair my garage door opener or replace it?

If the fix is a gear, a sensor, a remote, or an adjustment, repair is almost always the smarter spend. We lean toward replacement when the motor itself has failed, when the same unit keeps breaking down, or when an older opener lacks working safety reversal, since parts for discontinued models can be hard to source. Either way you get an honest recommendation and a flat-rate price before any work begins.

What kind of opener do I have: chain, belt, screw, or direct drive?

Look at the rail above your parked car. A metal chain like a bicycle's means chain drive, a smooth rubber belt means belt drive, and a long threaded steel rod means screw drive. If the motor itself travels along the rail instead of sitting in one spot, it is a direct drive. We repair all four types across Metro Detroit.
